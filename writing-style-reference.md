# Writing Style Reference Guide

*Elements of style from writers who shape how we communicate*

This document captures stylistic elements from writers whose work resonates with how we want to communicate: practical, human, narrative-driven, and accessible to business experts without dumbing things down.

**Core principle: More Paul Graham, less Tim Ferriss. No excessive hype or breathless excitement.**

---

## Voice Guidelines

### What We Avoid

- ❌ Breathless hype ("This changes EVERYTHING!")
- ❌ Excessive exclamation points and ALL CAPS
- ❌ Over-the-top claims ("Revolutionary! Game-changing! Unprecedented!")
- ❌ Artificial urgency ("Act NOW before it's too late!")
- ❌ Guru positioning ("I discovered the secret...")
- ❌ Marketing speak over substance

### What We Aim For

- ✅ Calm observation ("Here's what I'm seeing...")
- ✅ Substantive claims with evidence
- ✅ Matter-of-fact tone about interesting developments
- ✅ Letting the insights speak for themselves
- ✅ Intellectual curiosity over salesmanship
- ✅ Paul Graham's essay style: thoughtful, measured, insightful

## Core Influences

### Tim Ferriss

**What makes his style work:**

- **Deconstructionist approach** - Takes complex topics and breaks them into testable components
- **Pattern extraction** - "What do the best performers have in common?" mindset
- **Actionable specificity** - Not "work smarter," but "try this specific thing for 2 weeks"
- **Conversational authority** - Expert without being distant; accessible without being casual
- **Meta-awareness** - Comfortable saying "here's what I tried, here's what worked, here's what didn't"
- **Practical skepticism** - Questions conventional wisdom but with experiments, not just opinions

**Techniques to borrow:**

- Lead with the uncommon result ("most people do X, but what if Y?")
- Provide specific timeframes and constraints (not "eventually," but "within 3 weeks")
- Share both successes and failures transparently
- Use bullet points and formatting to aid scannability
- End sections with "try this" rather than "think about this"

**Voice characteristics:**
- Second person ("you") for engagement
- Short sentences for emphasis
- Questions that challenge assumptions
- Results-oriented language

---

### John McPhee (Tim Ferriss' Princeton writing professor)

**What makes his style work:**

- **Structure as storytelling** - Architecture of the piece IS the narrative
- **Concrete details that illuminate concepts** - A geologist's hammer tells you about the person
- **Patient scene-setting** - Doesn't rush to the point; lets you inhabit the moment
- **Character-driven complexity** - Shows ideas through people wrestling with them
- **Thematic braiding** - Weaves multiple threads that seem separate but illuminate each other
- **Research depth worn lightly** - Knows everything, shows just enough

**Techniques to borrow:**

- Open with a specific scene or moment, not an abstract claim
- Use physical objects and specific actions to reveal larger themes
- Let characters (real people) demonstrate complexity through their choices
- Structure pieces around a journey or process, not just a list of points
- Trust the reader to make connections; don't over-explain
- Return to opening images/scenes at the end for resonance

**Voice characteristics:**
- Third person observation with occasional "I" for positioning
- Longer sentences that accumulate detail
- Vivid present-tense scenes mixed with reflective passages
- Authoritative but not declarative

**Tim Ferriss on McPhee's teaching:**
> "He would take your draft, and he'd say, 'This is the piece. And here's the part that's interesting. Now, can you make the other 80% as interesting as this 20%?'"

---

### Bob Moesta (Jobs To Be Done)

**What makes his style work:**

- **Customer language, not company language** - How buyers actually describe their problems
- **Causality over correlation** - Not "what did they buy" but "what caused them to buy"
- **Push and pull forces** - The anxiety of new thing vs. the frustration of old thing
- **Progress-seeking frame** - People hire products to make progress in their lives
- **Struggle as insight** - The mess and contradiction in buying decisions is where truth lives
- **Timeline thinking** - First thought → active looking → first use → ongoing use

**Techniques to borrow:**

- Use buyer/customer quotes extensively (real voice, not sanitized)
- Map the emotional journey, not just the logical steps
- Identify what has to happen BEFORE the decision
- Frame problems as "helping people make progress" not "selling solutions"
- Show the competing forces (what holds them back + what pushes them forward)
- Distinguish between innovation (new to customer) and invention (new to world)

**Voice characteristics:**
- Conversational, like explaining to a colleague
- Lots of questions that reframe the problem
- "Here's what we learned" not "here's what you should do"
- Comfortable with messiness and contradiction

**Core frameworks to reference:**
- Jobs To Be Done (JTBD)
- Forces of Progress (push, pull, anxiety, habit)
- Timeline interviews (first thought → ongoing use)

### Clayton Christensen

**What makes his style work:**

- **Theory-building from observation** - Not just case studies, but "what explains this pattern?"
- **Paradox as starting point** - Why do good companies fail? Why does success cause failure?
- **Framework clarity** - Sustaining vs. disruptive innovation; not fuzzy concepts
- **Predictive utility** - These frameworks help you see what's coming
- **Business narrative as teaching tool** - Xerox, Digital Equipment, steel mills as characters
- **Moral seriousness** - Business decisions matter; they affect real people

**Techniques to borrow:**

- Start with the paradox or puzzle
- Build to a clear framework that explains multiple cases
- Use industry examples as evidence, not just illustration
- Show why conventional wisdom fails
- Provide a lens for evaluating what's happening now
- Connect business strategy to human impact

**Voice characteristics:**
- Professorial but accessible
- Patient explanation of complex dynamics
- Respectful toward companies that failed (they were rational, circumstances changed)
- Confident in frameworks without being dogmatic

**Key concepts to reference:**
- Disruptive innovation vs. sustaining innovation
- Jobs To Be Done (he popularized this)
- Overshooting customer needs
- Asymmetric motivation (why incumbents can't respond)

---

### Chris Koerner (Rapid iteration, shiny object balance)

**What makes his style work:**

- **Velocity over perfection** - Ship it, learn, iterate
- **Enthusiasm as competitive advantage** - Energy for new things drives experimentation
- **Low-commitment exploration** - Try it for a week, see what happens
- **Shiny object reframe** - Not distraction, but exploration at the edge
- **Bias toward action** - "Let's just try it" beats "let's think about trying it"
- **Playful seriousness** - Treats experimentation as both fun and strategic

**Techniques to borrow:**

- Give yourself permission to try things briefly
- Celebrate quick failures as learning (not waste)
- Use "what if?" questions to generate energy
- Balance depth (from others on this list) with breadth (Chris's strength)
- Frame rapid iteration as a methodology, not just a personality trait
- Show the value of 10 quick experiments vs. 1 perfect plan

**Voice characteristics:**
- High energy without being manic
- "This is cool, let me show you" enthusiasm
- Comfortable abandoning things that don't work
- Optimistic but not naive

**Role in our style:**
- Counterbalance to over-planning
- Permission to explore without justifying ROI upfront
- Reminds us experimentation is how you find what works

---

### C.S. Lewis

**What makes his style work:**

- **Everyday metaphors for profound ideas** - Describes spiritual reality like describing a landscape
- **Narrative as argument** - Story embeds theology without preaching
- **Intellectual accessibility** - Complex ideas in plain language
- **Sweeping scale grounded in specific detail** - From cosmic to intimate without whiplash
- **Both/and rather than either/or** - Human AND spiritual; rational AND imaginative
- **Conversational authority** - Feels like a conversation with someone wiser, not a lecture
- **Moral clarity without sanctimony** - Right and wrong matter, but he's not hectoring

**Techniques to borrow:**

- Use analogies from everyday experience (not jargon)
- Paint scenes that let readers experience the concept
- Move between big-picture (cosmic scale) and intimate (personal moment)
- Trust that readers can handle complexity if you guide them
- Don't shy from big claims, but ground them in specifics
- Write as if speaking to a friend who takes ideas seriously

**Voice characteristics:**
- First person when personal experience illuminates the point
- Direct address ("you") when inviting reader into shared experience
- Precise word choice without being ornate
- Sentences that breathe (varied rhythm, not all short or all long)

**What we're NOT borrowing:**
- Explicitly religious framing (we're not writing theology)
- 1940s British cultural references

**What we ARE borrowing:**
- Scale (from individual choice to industry transformation)
- Human + technical synthesis (business tools AND human capability)
- Narrative that embeds argument
- Clear moral stakes without preaching (this matters for people's careers)

## Additional Influences (Validated)

### Paul Graham (Essays) ✓ CONFIRMED

**Why he fits:**

- **Thinking out loud on the page** - Not polished pronouncements, but exploration
- **Founder/builder perspective** - Writes from doing, not observing
- **Contrarian but not reflexively so** - Challenges consensus when he has reason to
- **Tight prose** - No wasted words, but not terse
- **"What if?" questions that reframe** - "What if we stopped doing X?"

**Techniques:**
- Start with a question or counterintuitive claim
- Build argument step by step (doesn't skip logic)
- Use programming/building analogies (compounding, iteration, abstraction)
- Personal anecdotes as evidence
- End with implications, not just restatement

**Voice characteristics:**
- First person reflection
- Conversational but precise
- Comfortable with strong claims backed by reasoning
- Founder's pragmatism

**Key essays to reference:**
- "How to Do Great Work" (2023)
- "Maker's Schedule, Manager's Schedule" (2009)
- "The Age of the Essay" (2004)
- "Schlep Blindness" (2012)

---

### Derek Sivers ✓ CONFIRMED

**Why he fits:**

- **Brevity as respect** - Say it in 200 words, not 2000
- **Narrative minimalism** - One point, one story, done
- **Unexpected angles** - Finds the non-obvious insight
- **Aphoristic without being gimmicky** - Memorable phrases that carry meaning
- **Generosity of spirit** - Shares what worked without claiming it's universal

**Techniques:**
- Shorter than you think necessary
- Lead with the insight, explain after
- Use "I" for personal experience, "you" for application
- End with a question or invitation to think differently
- Strip backstory unless it serves the point

**Voice characteristics:**
- Warm, almost conversational
- Not trying to impress
- Comfortable with simplicity
- Playful wisdom

**Best use cases for our work:**
- LinkedIn posts (short, punchy insights)
- Email sequences (one idea, clearly stated)
- Article intros (hook with unexpected angle)
- CTAs (end with question/invitation)

**Key pieces to reference:**
- "There's no speed limit" (sive.rs/kimo)
- "Obvious to you. Amazing to others" (sive.rs/obvious)
- "Hell Yeah or No" collection

### William Zinsser (On Writing Well) ✓ CONFIRMED (PRINCIPLES)

**Why he fits:**

- **Clarity above all** - Strip every unnecessary word
- **Trust the reader** - Don't explain what's already clear
- **Humanity in nonfiction** - Even technical writing has a human behind it
- **Rewriting is writing** - First draft gets thoughts down, revision makes them clear

**Techniques:**
- Cut relentlessly (every word must earn its place)
- Use active voice unless passive serves a purpose
- Read aloud to catch clunky phrasing
- Start strong (don't clear your throat for three paragraphs)

**Voice characteristics:**
- Direct and unpretentious
- Respectful toward reader's intelligence and time
- Warm without being folksy

**How to use:**
- Editing framework (not style guide)
- "Can I cut 20%?" test after first draft
- Voice check: "Would I say this in conversation?"
- Clarity check: "What's this paragraph actually saying?"

**Key chapters to reference:**
- Chapter 2: "Simplicity"
- Chapter 5: "The Audience"
- Chapter 12: "Writing About People: The Interview"

### Anne Lamott (Bird by Bird) ✓ CONCEPTS ONLY

**Why concepts fit (style doesn't):**

- **Permission to write badly first** - "Shitty first drafts" = iterate messily = scrappy hacking
- **Small assignments** - Bird by bird = one piece at a time = avoid overwhelm
- **Process transparency** - Show the rough edges, not just polished results
- **Humor about anxiety** - "Am I technical enough?" = Radio Station KFKD

**Techniques to borrow:**
- Give readers permission to experiment messily
- "Here's what I tried first, here's what actually worked"
- Break overwhelming tasks into small pieces
- Use humor to defuse impostor syndrome
- Normalize the struggle

**What we're NOT borrowing:**
- Personal/emotional memoir style
- Fiction-focused examples
- Spiritual/12-step references

**Application to our work:**
- "Not a real developer" = permission-giving
- "Scrappy hacking" = shitty first drafts applied to code
- "Start with side projects" = bird by bird approach

## Conceptual References (Not Style Influences)

These writers have frameworks/concepts worth referencing, but we're not borrowing their writing style.

### James Clear (Atomic Habits)

**Why concepts matter:**

Adaptation IS habit change. Moving from traditional BI to AI+IDE requires:
- Identity shifts ("I'm not a developer" → "I'm a business expert who builds with AI")
- Environment design (IDE setup, workspace structure, daily practices)
- Small wins compound (one automation → systematic capability)
- Systems over goals (build the practice, not just one project)

**Key concepts to reference:**

1. **Habit stacking** - "After I finish my morning Power BI report review, I spend 15 minutes on an AI+IDE experiment"

2. **Identity-based habits** - "The goal is not to learn Python. The goal is to become someone who solves business problems with AI assistance."

3. **Environment design** - Set up your IDE workspace to make experimentation easy (templates, saved contexts, example projects visible)

4. **1% better every day** - Small experiments compound. Not "become a developer," but "try one thing today"

5. **Make it obvious/easy/attractive/satisfying** - The four laws applied to learning AI+IDE

6. **Atomic = small + part of larger system** - One automation is atomic. It's small enough to complete AND it's part of becoming AI-capable.

**How to reference in our content:**

✓ "Like atomic habits, atomic automations. Start small. Let them compound."

✓ "This isn't about becoming a developer. It's about identity shift: from 'I build reports' to 'I build solutions.'"

✓ "Environment matters. Set up your IDE so experimentation is easier than not experimenting."

✓ "You don't need a transformation plan. You need one small win that proves it works."

**What we're NOT doing:**
- ❌ Imitating his writing style (clear but generic)
- ❌ Over-relying on habits framework (it's one lens, not the only lens)
- ❌ Making it too prescriptive (he leans behavioral, we lean experiential)

**Why excluded as style influence:**

Writing style is functional but not distinctive. Gets the job done without being memorable. Voice doesn't match our "scrappy hacker discovering breakthrough" energy. But the concepts? Incredibly useful for our audience.

**Personal note:**

You've read Atomic Habits 5x (more than any other modern book in your library). That's signal. The concepts resonate deeply because they map directly to how people actually change behavior. Reference the frameworks, just not the writing style.

## Style Elements We're Cultivating

Based on these influences, here are the elements we're actively cultivating in our own voice:

### 1. Practical Authority
- Expert knowledge worn lightly
- "Here's what I've learned" not "here's what you must do"
- Specific examples > abstract principles
- Comfortable saying "this worked" and "this didn't"

### 2. Narrative Scaffolding
- Structure through story (journey, process, timeline)
- Real people and real moments, not just concepts
- Scenes that let readers inhabit the experience
- Return to opening images for resonance

### 3. Accessible Complexity
- Don't dumb down, but don't show off either
- Metaphors from everyday experience
- Build complexity step by step
- Trust readers to make connections

### 4. Conversational Precision
- Clear and direct, not casual and sloppy
- Active voice unless passive serves a purpose
- Varied sentence rhythm (short for punch, longer for flow)
- Read-aloud test (does it sound human?)

### 5. Experimental Mindset
- Velocity and iteration over perfection
- Permission to try things and abandon what doesn't work
- "Let's see what happens" as legitimate strategy
- Quick failures as learning, not waste

### 6. Moral Seriousness Without Preaching
- This stuff matters (people's careers, business outcomes)
- Respect for everyone in the story
- Clear stakes without melodrama
- Right and wrong exist, but we're not hectoring

### 7. Meta-Awareness
- Comfortable showing the process ("here's how I'm thinking about this")
- Transparent about what's working and what's not
- Recursive observation (thinking about thinking)
- Credit to others whose ideas we're building on

### 8. Human + Technical Integration
- Business problems AND technical solutions
- Strategic thinking AND practical implementation
- People AND tools
- Not "soft skills vs. hard skills" but both/and

## What We're Avoiding

Based on these influences, here's what we're explicitly NOT doing:

### ❌ Corporate Blandness
- "Leverage synergies" "drive value" "thought leadership"
- Passive voice hiding who's doing what
- Jargon that obscures rather than clarifies
- Playing it safe to avoid offending anyone

### ❌ Academic Distance
- Third person omniscient when first person is clearer
- Hedge words ("perhaps," "arguably," "to some extent")
- Complexity for its own sake
- Hiding behind citations rather than making claims

### ❌ Hype and Breathlessness
- "Revolutionary!" "Game-changing!" "Unprecedented!"
- Every sentence ending with an exclamation point!
- Overpromising to generate clicks
- Fear-mongering about what you'll miss

### ❌ Guru Posturing
- "I've discovered the one true way"
- Belittling people who don't get it yet
- Gatekeeping knowledge
- Selling the dream rather than the method

### ❌ Apologetic Hedging
- "This might not work for everyone..."
- "I'm no expert, but..."
- Six paragraphs of throat-clearing before the point
- Undermining your own authority preemptively

## Voice Checklist

When reviewing a piece, check against these criteria:

**Clarity:**
- [ ] Could I read this aloud without stumbling?
- [ ] Are technical terms explained when first used?
- [ ] Does the structure help or hinder understanding?
- [ ] Can I cut 20% without losing meaning?

**Authority:**
- [ ] Am I speaking from experience, not speculation?
- [ ] Have I provided specific examples?
- [ ] Do I acknowledge what I don't know?
- [ ] Am I making claims I can back up?

**Humanity:**
- [ ] Does this sound like a person, not a corporation?
- [ ] Are there real people in this story?
- [ ] Have I acknowledged complexity and contradiction?
- [ ] Would I say this to someone's face?

**Utility:**
- [ ] Can someone DO something with this?
- [ ] Are next steps clear and actionable?
- [ ] Have I balanced theory with practice?
- [ ] Is the "so what?" obvious?

**Engagement:**
- [ ] Would I keep reading if I wasn't the author?
- [ ] Does the opening hook?
- [ ] Does the pace vary (not monotonous)?
- [ ] Does it end strongly?

## How to Use This Document

**When starting a new piece:**
1. Scan the influences most relevant to this piece's purpose
2. Pick 2-3 techniques to explicitly try
3. Review the voice checklist before you start

**When revising:**
1. Read aloud (catches awkwardness)
2. Check against "What We're Avoiding"
3. Run through voice checklist
4. Cut 20%, see if it's stronger

**When stuck:**
1. Pick a writer from this list
2. Read one of their pieces
3. Notice what they're doing technically
4. Try that technique in your own voice

**When collaborating with AI:**
1. Reference specific writers/techniques in prompts
2. Ask AI to explain its reasoning against these criteria
3. Validate output against voice checklist
4. Remember: AI can mimic techniques, but voice requires human judgment

## The Three-Draft Process: How to Actually Use These Influences

Different writers serve different stages. Here's the pattern:

### Draft 1: Get It Down (Permission & Structure)

**Goal:** Get thinking onto the page without self-editing

**Active influences:**
- **Anne Lamott** - "Shitty first drafts" permission. Write badly, write fast, write messy.
- **Chris Koerner** - Velocity over perfection. Try ideas. See what lands.
- **Paul Graham** - Think out loud. Let the reasoning emerge as you write.

**What you're doing:**
- Brain dump the core ideas
- Follow your curiosity (might not be linear)
- Include too much rather than too little
- Write in your natural voice (don't perform)
- Note where you need examples/evidence but keep moving

**What you're NOT doing:**
- ❌ Editing sentences
- ❌ Checking voice consistency  
- ❌ Worrying about structure
- ❌ Cutting anything (yet)

**Output:** 
Messy document with good ideas poorly expressed. Probably 30% longer than final will be. Some sections sharp, others rambling. That's perfect.

**Time:** 60-90 minutes for ~1,500-2,000 word piece

---

### Draft 2: Find The Structure (Architecture & Flow)

**Goal:** Discover what you're actually saying and build clean architecture

**Active influences:**
- **John McPhee** - What's the architecture? What's the narrative spine?
- **Bob Moesta** - What's the customer struggle? What progress are they trying to make?
- **Clayton Christensen** - What's the framework or pattern that explains the examples?
- **Paul Graham** - Does the argument build step-by-step? Any logical gaps?

**What you're doing:**
- Identify the 3-5 core points (might not be what you thought you were writing about)
- Reorder sections for logical flow
- Cut entire sections that don't serve the thesis
- Add section headers that signal structure
- Strengthen the opening (what's the question/paradox/puzzle?)
- Make sure each section connects to the next
- Add examples where reasoning feels abstract
- Note which concepts need James Clear frameworks or other conceptual references

**Architecture questions to ask:**

1. **Opening:** Does it establish why this matters? (Tim Ferriss: uncommon result)
2. **Structure:** Does each section build on the previous? (Paul Graham: step-by-step logic)
3. **Evidence:** Do I show examples or just assert? (McPhee: concrete details, Moesta: customer quotes)
4. **Narrative:** Is there a journey/process/timeline? (McPhee: thematic braiding)
5. **Payoff:** Do the implications/applications come through clearly? (Christensen: predictive utility)

**What you're NOT doing:**
- ❌ Sentence-level editing (yet)
- ❌ Perfecting word choice
- ❌ Final polish

**Output:**
Clean structure with rough prose. The ideas make sense in sequence. Thesis is clear. Examples support points. But sentences still clunky, transitions rough, some redundancy remains.

**Time:** 45-60 minutes

---

### Draft 3: Make It Tight & Human (Clarity & Voice)

**Goal:** Strip waste, sharpen voice, make every sentence earn its place

**Active influences:**
- **William Zinsser** - Cut 20%. Make every word earn its place. Active voice default.
- **Tim Ferriss** - Actionable specificity. Clear next steps. Scannable formatting.
- **Derek Sivers** - Can this be shorter? Lead with insight, explain after.
- **C.S. Lewis** - Does it breathe? Vary sentence rhythm. Trust the reader.

**What you're doing:**

**Clarity pass:**
- Cut redundancy (same point made multiple times)
- Eliminate throat-clearing ("It's worth noting that..." "One could argue..." "In today's world...")
- Replace weak verbs + adverbs with strong verbs (very important → critical)
- Change passive to active voice (unless passive serves a purpose)
- Read aloud - fix anything that makes you stumble
- Test: Can you cut 20% without losing meaning?

**Voice pass:**
- Remove AI-generic language ("unlock," "leverage," "game-changing")
- Add conversational markers where appropriate ("Here's what's wild:", "Let me show you")
- Check: Would you say this in conversation? If not, rewrite.
- Vary sentence length (short for punch, medium for flow, occasional long for building complexity)
- Make sure examples sound like real people (Moesta: customer language, not company language)

**Utility pass:**
- Make next steps explicit (Ferriss: actionable specificity)
- Add formatting for scannability (bold key phrases, bullets, short paragraphs)
- Strengthen the ending (clear implication or invitation)
- Check section headers - do they signal value?

**Final checks:**
- Opening: Does first paragraph hook?
- Structure: Can you skim headers and get the story?
- Voice: Does it sound like you?
- Utility: Can someone DO something with this?
- Length: Is every word necessary?

**What you're NOT doing:**
- ❌ Adding new ideas (structure is locked)
- ❌ Second-guessing the thesis
- ❌ Major reorganization

**Output:**
Tight, clear, human-sounding piece that delivers value. Ready to publish.

**Time:** 45-60 minutes

## The AI Collaboration Twist

Here's what's different when working with AI in IDE:

### Draft 1 with AI:
- **You:** Brain dump core ideas, rough structure, key points
- **AI:** Extend thinking, add related points, challenge gaps
- **You:** Validate reasoning, keep what aligns, cut what doesn't

### Draft 2 with AI:
- **You:** Identify what you're actually saying (thesis discovery)
- **AI:** Reorganize structure, suggest transitions, find logical gaps
- **You:** Accept architecture changes, ensure voice stays yours

### Draft 3 with AI:
- **You:** Mark sections that feel clunky/verbose/unclear
- **AI:** Suggest tighter phrasing, cut redundancy, improve flow
- **You:** Accept word-level changes that preserve your voice, reject generic AI-isms

**Critical rule:** 
AI can suggest at any stage, but YOU validate against voice. The style influences are your validation checklist.

**Example validation:**
- AI suggests: "This innovative approach leverages cutting-edge technology"
- You check: Would Zinsser approve? (No - pompous frills) Would I say this? (No)
- You revise: "This works because AI maintains context across multiple files"

## The "When Am I Done?" Test

Run through this checklist before calling it finished:

### Clarity (Zinsser):
- [ ] Could I read this aloud without stumbling?
- [ ] Can I cut 20% without losing meaning? (If yes, cut more)
- [ ] Are technical terms explained when first used?
- [ ] Active voice unless passive serves a purpose?

### Authority (Ferriss, Graham, Christensen):
- [ ] Am I speaking from experience, not speculation?
- [ ] Have I provided specific examples?
- [ ] Do I acknowledge what I don't know?
- [ ] Are claims backed by reasoning or evidence?

### Structure (McPhee, Graham):
- [ ] Does opening establish why this matters?
- [ ] Does each section build on previous?
- [ ] Can someone skim headers and get the story?
- [ ] Does ending provide clear implication or next step?

### Humanity (Lewis, Moesta, Sivers):
- [ ] Does this sound like a person, not a corporation?
- [ ] Are there real people/moments in this story?
- [ ] Would I say this to someone's face?
- [ ] Have I acknowledged complexity and contradiction?

### Utility (Ferriss, Moesta):
- [ ] Can someone DO something with this?
- [ ] Are next steps clear and actionable?
- [ ] Have I balanced theory with practice?
- [ ] Is the "so what?" obvious?

### Voice (All influences):
- [ ] Would I keep reading if I wasn't the author?
- [ ] Does this sound like me?
- [ ] Have I avoided AI-generic language?
- [ ] Does the pace vary (not monotonous)?

**If you can check all boxes:** Ship it.

**If not:** One more pass on whatever's weak.

## Emergency Fixes: When Something's Wrong But You Don't Know What

### "This feels boring"
- **Check:** Opening. Does it hook? (Ferriss: uncommon result, Graham: counterintuitive question)
- **Check:** Examples. Are they specific and real? (McPhee: concrete details, Moesta: real quotes)
- **Check:** Stakes. Does it matter? (Lewis: moral clarity, Christensen: real impact)

### "This feels too long"
- **Apply:** Zinsser cut test (20% without losing meaning)
- **Apply:** Sivers brevity test (can this be half as long?)
- **Check:** Redundancy. Am I making the same point multiple times?

### "This doesn't sound like me"
- **Check:** AI-generic language. Remove "leverage," "unlock," "game-changing"
- **Check:** Conversational test. Would I say this out loud? (Zinsser)
- **Check:** Voice consistency. Does Draft 1 sound more like me? Roll back to that tone.

### "The structure feels wrong"
- **Apply:** McPhee architecture question. What's the narrative spine?
